Living Upstream — Book 2: Application & Integration

Chapter 1 — Upstream Thinking at Work

Work is where most people feel the downstream pull strongest. Deadlines, endless emails, office politics, and reactive problem-solving can drain energy, scatter focus, and reduce clarity. Upstream thinking at work is about **steering your attention and energy intentionally**, applying the principles of upstream living in professional environments to maximize impact and maintain flow.

The first step is awareness. Observe your patterns: what consumes your attention, which tasks drain energy, and where downstream currents appear. Upstream thinking reframes work not as a series of crises to react to, but as a system of currents you can navigate with intention.

Exercise: Work Energy Audit

For one week, track your energy and focus throughout the workday. Note the activities, meetings, emails, and interactions that either elevate or deplete energy. Use this awareness to design **upstream interventions** — adjustments to schedule, communication style, or task prioritization.

Story: The River Engineer

Imagine an engineer shaping a river to prevent flooding and improve irrigation. She cannot control the water entirely but can redirect currents, build levees, and reinforce banks. Similarly, at work, you cannot control all external demands, but you can manage your attention, prioritize tasks, and create upstream structures that make flow predictable and sustainable.

Some upstream practices for work include:

  • Batching similar tasks to reduce context-switching
  • Scheduling focused work blocks and breaks
  • Communicating proactively rather than reactively
  • Creating systems for repeated tasks to reduce friction
  • Reflecting at day’s end on wins, drains, and next-day intentions

Reflection

Observe how much of your work is reactive versus proactive. Which small changes could shift your daily flow upstream? Awareness plus micro-adjustments compounds over time into clarity, efficiency, and energy preservation.

Extended Exercise: Upstream Task Mapping

Draw a map of your weekly tasks. Categorize them as:

  • High impact, upstream tasks — drive meaningful results
  • Necessary but low impact tasks — schedule efficiently
  • Downstream distractions — minimize, delegate, or remove

Use this map to prioritize energy and attention. Focus first on upstream tasks and structure your week around them, creating flow that naturally resists downstream chaos.

Practical Examples

  • Start the morning with the highest-impact task, before checking emails
  • Schedule a short reflection mid-day to reassess priorities
  • Use templates or automation for repetitive communication
  • End the day with a 10-minute review of what flowed upstream and what did not

Reflection Questions

  • Which recurring tasks pull you downstream most?
  • How can you batch, automate, or delegate them to maintain upstream flow?
  • Which small upstream interventions could compound over the next week?

Micro-Action Plan

Tomorrow, select one upstream intervention to implement at work — batch a task, schedule a focus block, or streamline communication. Track its impact on energy, focus, and results. Each micro-adjustment compounds, gradually transforming your workdays into predictable, upstream flows where energy and attention amplify impact.
